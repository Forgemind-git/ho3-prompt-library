Blog Post Outline

Open Claude.ai →
Create a SEO-structured blog post outline with H2/H3 hierarchy, estimated section word counts, and notes for each section — so a writer can produce a complete draft without needing to think about structure, and every section earns its place.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Topic: [blog post topic]. Target keyword: [primary keyword]. Search intent: [informational / commercial / navigational]. Target audience: [who reads this and what problem are they solving]. Brand: [company name, voice — e.g., "authoritative but not stuffy"].
Role You are an SEO content strategist who builds outlines that rank AND convert — you don't optimise for Google at the expense of the reader, or for the reader at the expense of ranking.
Instruction Create a detailed blog post outline with: SEO title options (3), intro hook approach, H2 sections with H3 sub-sections, section notes, and a conclusion with CTA approach.
Spec Outline format. Total target word count: [800 / 1200 / 2000 words]. Each H2 has an estimated word count allocation. Section notes tell the writer what to include, not how to write it.
Performance A writer with no additional briefing can produce a complete first draft from this outline. The keyword appears in the title, meta description hint, and at least 2 H2s naturally.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm a content marketing manager at [Company Name], a [brief company description — what you do and for whom]. I'm writing a blog post on: [topic]. 
- Primary target keyword: [keyword]
- Secondary keywords: [list 2–3]
- Search intent: [informational — "how to X" / commercial — "best X" / comparison — "X vs Y"]
- Target audience: [describe who reads this and what problem they're trying to solve]
- Ideal reader outcome: [what should the reader be able to do or understand after reading?]
- Brand voice: [describe tone — e.g., "practical, direct, no fluff; expertise without jargon"]
- Word count target: [800 / 1200 / 2000 words]
- Any competitors ranking for this topic I should be aware of: [list or "I'll handle this separately"]

Role: Act as an SEO content strategist who builds outlines that rank for the target keyword AND genuinely help the reader — not keyword-stuffed content that embarrasses the brand.

Instruction: Create a blog post outline with:
1. Three SEO title options — one question-based, one "how to", one direct/list format
2. Meta description hint — one sentence summary for the writer to refine
3. Intro — describe the hook approach (problem / statistic / story / counter-intuitive claim) and what the intro must accomplish
4. H2 Sections — 4–6 H2s with: title, estimated word count, H3 sub-sections where needed, and 2–3 sentence notes for the writer
5. Conclusion — approach for the closing section and CTA type

Spec:
- Outline format (not a draft — section notes only)
- Word count must add up to the target
- Primary keyword in at least 2 H2 titles naturally
- Section notes: what to cover, not how to write it
- Include a "what to avoid" note for the most common mistake in this type of post

Performance bar: Writer produces a complete, on-target draft from this outline without any additional briefing. Keyword appears naturally (not forced) in title, 2 H2s, and first paragraph.

1. Introduction 2. What are project management tools? 3. Top tools 4. How to choose 5. Conclusion

Email Newsletter Draft

Open Claude.ai →
Draft a focused email newsletter with a clear editorial theme, one primary CTA, and a voice that readers actually want to receive — not a content dump of everything you published this month.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Newsletter: [name and description]. Frequency: [weekly / bi-weekly / monthly]. Audience: [who subscribes and why]. This edition's theme: [the topic or angle for this issue]. Content to include: [links, stories, tips, product news]. Primary CTA: [what you want readers to do].
Role You are an email editor who knows that a newsletter with one clear editorial point of view outperforms a newsletter that "covers everything." You write editions people forward to colleagues.
Instruction Draft a newsletter with: a subject line that earns the open, a focused editorial opening, the main content section, and one clear CTA — not five different things to click.
Spec Email format. 350–450 words of body copy. One subject line + one pre-header. One primary CTA (not three). Scannable — short paragraphs, one image placeholder noted, one pull quote.
Performance Reader opens, reads to the end, and takes the one action you wanted them to take. The newsletter has a clear point of view, not a content digest. Open rate potential: the subject line is specific enough to stand out.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm writing the [edition number / date] edition of [Newsletter Name], a [frequency] newsletter for [audience description]. 

This edition's theme: [the single editorial focus for this issue]

Content to include:
- Main story/angle: [describe the main piece of content or idea you want to lead with]
- Secondary item 1: [link, story, or tip]
- Secondary item 2: [optional]
- Product/company news to weave in: [optional — keep brief]

Primary CTA: [what you want the reader to do — one thing only]
Tone/voice: [describe the newsletter's voice — e.g., "curious, slightly irreverent, always evidence-backed"]
Previous edition topic: [so this one feels like a continuation, not a repeat]

Role: Act as an email editor who writes newsletters with a clear editorial voice and one main idea per edition — not content aggregators disguised as newsletters.

Instruction: Write a complete newsletter draft with:
1. Subject line — specific enough to stand out in a busy inbox; not clickbait; includes the main idea
2. Pre-header text — 40–60 characters, extends the subject line
3. Opening — 2–3 sentences that set the editorial theme and give the reader a reason to keep reading; no "Welcome to this week's edition of..."
4. Main Content — the primary story/angle, 200–250 words, with the main insight clearly stated
5. Quick Takes — 1–2 secondary items as brief callouts (50 words each max)
6. CTA Section — one clear, specific action with a short explanation of why this matters
7. Sign-off — brief, human, consistent with the newsletter's voice

Spec:
- 350–450 words body copy
- One subject line + one pre-header
- One primary CTA
- No "click here" — specific link text
- Short paragraphs (3 sentences max)
- One [IMAGE PLACEHOLDER: description] note

Performance bar: Reader reads to the end. The subject line is specific enough that they know what they're getting before they open. One clear action they took after reading. The newsletter has a POV, not a topic list.

Case Study Interview Questions

Open Claude.ai →
Generate targeted, open-ended interview questions for a customer case study that surface specific outcomes, memorable quotes, and emotional moments — rather than producing a story full of vague claims like "we saved time" with no proof.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Customer: [company name, industry, size]. What they use your product for: [use case]. Known outcome: [metrics you already know — e.g., "they reduced support tickets by 40%"]. Case study angle: [what story you want to tell]. Intended audience for the case study: [who will read it — prospects, specific role].
Role You are a B2B content writer who has conducted 100+ customer interviews. You know that the best case study quotes come from questions about the before-state, the decision moment, and the specific moment things changed — not from asking "what do you like about our product?"
Instruction Generate 15 interview questions organised by phase: before (the problem), the decision, implementation, results, and emotional/personal. Prioritise questions that will surface quotable answers and specific numbers.
Spec 15 questions organised into 5 phases (3 questions each). Each question is open-ended (no yes/no). Include a note on what each question is designed to surface. 300 words max for the question set.
Performance The interviewer uses these 15 questions and walks away with at least 3 quotable moments and 2 specific metrics. No question can be answered with "yes" or "no."

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm conducting a case study interview with [Customer Name/Company] for [Your Company]'s case study program. 
- Customer: [company name], [industry], [company size]
- What they use our product for: [primary use case]
- Known outcomes we want to confirm/expand: [metrics you already know or suspect — e.g., "we know they saved 40% on support costs; we want the story behind it"]
- Angle for this case study: [the narrative hook — e.g., "from reactive to proactive support" / "scaling without hiring" / "migration success story"]
- Intended audience for the case study: [e.g., "VP-level CS leaders at Series B SaaS companies"]
- What the customer has agreed to share: [public name and logo / anonymised / specific metrics only]

Role: Act as an experienced B2B case study interviewer who knows that the best quotes and moments come from asking about the before-state, the turning point, and specific moments — not from asking customers to describe your product's features.

Instruction: Generate 15 interview questions organised into 5 phases:
1. The Before (3 questions) — surface the pain, the workarounds, the cost of not solving it
2. The Decision (3 questions) — the evaluation process, what tipped the decision
3. Implementation (3 questions) — what the rollout was like, surprises, team reaction
4. Results (3 questions) — specific outcomes, what changed, what they measure now
5. Emotional/Personal (3 questions) — moments of pride, recommendation, the human story

Spec:
- 15 questions, 5 phases, 3 per phase
- Each question: open-ended only (no yes/no possible)
- Include one-line note on what each question is designed to surface
- 300 words for the question set
- No question should include your product name (makes answers less authentic)

Performance bar: Interviewer walks away with 3+ quotable moments and 2+ specific metrics. Every question could produce a usable quote. No yes/no answers possible.

Before — Weak prompt output

1. What do you think of our product? 2. What features do you use most? 3. Would you recommend us? 4. What results have you seen? 5. Any other feedback?

Social Media Repurpose Pack

Open Claude.ai →
Turn one long-form content piece into 5 platform-specific social media posts, each adapted for the norms of that platform — so one piece of content creates a week of social output without being copy-pasted five times.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Source content: [link or paste the main content piece]. Platforms to target: [LinkedIn / Twitter/X / Instagram / Facebook / Threads]. Brand: [company name, brand voice]. Campaign period: [when to post these].
Role You are a social media content specialist who knows that the same content on five platforms should feel native to each, not like a press release distributed everywhere.
Instruction Adapt the source content into 5 platform-specific posts, each using the conventions of that platform: LinkedIn (professional, paragraph-heavy), Twitter/X (punchy, under 280 characters or a thread), Instagram (visual hook, caption with hashtags), Facebook (community-focused), Threads (conversational, short).
Spec 5 posts, one per platform. Each must feel native to the platform format. Include character count where relevant. Include 3–5 suggested hashtags for Instagram only.
Performance Each post would be appropriate to schedule without further editing. Posts don't feel like they came from the same template. The main insight from the source piece is preserved in all five but expressed differently.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm repurposing [content type — e.g., "a blog post / podcast episode / case study / webinar recap"] about [topic summary] for [Company Name]'s social channels. Brand voice: [describe — e.g., "practical, direct, evidence-driven; we don't use jargon or inspirational platitudes"].

Source content summary (paste a summary OR the key insight/stats/quotes you want to use):
[Paste 3–5 key points, stats, or quotes from the source content]

Target platforms: [LinkedIn / Twitter-X / Instagram / Facebook / Threads — list which ones you want]
Campaign timing: [when these will be posted]
CTA or destination link: [URL to send people to, if applicable]

Role: Act as a social media content specialist who understands that LinkedIn readers want depth, Twitter/X readers want a punchy take, and Instagram audiences need a visual hook. You write each platform natively, not as a copy-paste job.

Instruction: Write one post for each platform listed, following these conventions:
- LinkedIn: 150–250 words, paragraph format, one insight per post, close with a genuine question or one CTA
- Twitter/X: Either one punchy post under 280 characters OR a 4-tweet thread where each tweet can stand alone
- Instagram: Short visual hook (1–2 lines) + 100–150 word caption + 5 relevant hashtags
- Facebook: 100–150 words, community/discussion framing, question to encourage comments
- Threads: 2–3 short posts (under 500 chars each) in a conversational chain

Spec:
- Each post clearly labelled by platform
- Character counts shown for Twitter/X and Threads
- Instagram hashtags separated at end of caption
- No post should feel like it was translated from another platform
- All use the key insight from the source content but frame it differently

Performance bar: Any post from this pack can be scheduled without editing. Each feels native to its platform. The main insight is in all five but expressed in ways appropriate to each audience.

Before — Weak prompt output

Post for all channels: "Check out our new blog post about customer support for small teams! We share 5 tips to help you manage support without a big team. Link in bio! #customerservice #smallbusiness #tips"

SEO Meta Description

Open Claude.ai →
Write SEO-optimised meta descriptions and title tags that stay within Google's character limits, include the target keyword naturally, and are written to earn a click — not just describe the page.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Page: [URL or page description]. Target keyword: [primary keyword]. Search intent: [what the searcher wants — informational / commercial / navigational]. Current meta (if any): [paste current meta to improve].
Role You are an SEO specialist who writes meta descriptions that treat the search result page as a conversion moment — you're competing against 9 other results and every word counts.
Instruction Write 3 variations each of: title tag and meta description. Each variation must include the target keyword, stay within character limits, and use a different click-trigger (curiosity, specificity, urgency, benefit).
Spec Title tag: 50–60 characters max. Meta description: 150–160 characters max. Keyword in first 30 characters of title tag. 3 variations of each. Character count shown next to each.
Performance All 6 pieces stay within character limits (checked). Keyword is in all 6 pieces. At least one variation for each uses a different click motivator. A/B testable.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm writing SEO meta tags for a page at [URL or description of page content]. 
- Target keyword: [primary keyword]
- Search intent: [informational / commercial / navigational / transactional]
- Page content summary: [2–3 sentences describing what the page covers]
- Brand name to include: [company name — include in title tag or not?]
- Current meta description (if updating): [paste current version or "none"]
- Top competitor title/meta example (optional): [paste a competitor's to differentiate from]

Instruction: Write 3 variations of each:
1. Title Tag — 3 variations (50–60 characters each)
   - Variation A: Leads with the keyword, benefits-focused
   - Variation B: Question or "how to" format
   - Variation C: Specificity/number format
2. Meta Description — 3 variations (150–160 characters each)
   - Variation A: Benefit-led
   - Variation B: Problem/solution format
   - Variation C: Urgency or specificity

Spec:
- Title tag: 50–60 characters max (show character count in brackets)
- Meta description: 150–160 characters max (show character count in brackets)
- Keyword must appear in all 6 pieces
- Each variation uses a different click motivator (not the same opening word)
- Show character count next to each piece

Performance bar: All pieces are within character limits. Keyword is in all 6. Each pair of title + meta variation works together. A/B testable — variations are meaningfully different, not just word-swap.

Competitor Content Gap Analysis

Open Claude.ai →
Identify content topics and keywords that competitors rank for (or cover well) that you don't — and prioritise them by estimated traffic potential and conversion relevance to your ICP.

CRISP Breakdown

Context Your company: [name, what you do]. Competitors: [list 2–4 competitors]. Your ICP: [who your ideal customer is]. Your current content strengths: [what topics you already cover well].
Role You are a content strategist who runs gap analyses that produce a prioritised content roadmap — not a list of every topic competitors mention, but the 10 topics worth actually creating.
Instruction Analyse the competitor content information provided and produce: top 10 gap topics with search intent, estimated priority (high/medium/low), and a content angle that would differentiate your version from the competitor's.
Spec Gap analysis table format. Each topic: keyword or topic, current owner (which competitor), search intent, priority, your differentiated angle. 10 topics, prioritised by high to low.
Performance Content team can take this table and start briefing the top 3 pieces this week. Each differentiated angle is specific enough to be a content brief hook.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I'm running a content gap analysis for [Company Name] ([what you do, 1 sentence]) against these competitors: [list competitor names and URLs]. Our ICP: [describe your ideal customer — industry, role, company size, pain point]. Our existing content strengths (topics we already cover well): [list 3–5 topics or categories].

Competitor content information (paste what you know — from SEMrush, Ahrefs, manual research, or just the topics you've seen them cover):
[Paste competitor blog topics, categories, or keyword data here]

Role: Act as a content strategist who turns gap analysis data into a prioritised content roadmap — not a list of everything competitors do, but the 10 topics most worth creating for your ICP.

Instruction: Based on the competitor information provided, identify the top 10 content gaps — topics or keywords that competitors are covering that you are not. For each:
1. Topic/Keyword — the content gap

- Table format

- Your Angle must be specific — not "write a comprehensive guide"

Write a post-webinar follow-up email sequence that thanks attendees, delivers the promised content, drives the intended next step, and nurtures no-shows — without being a generic "thanks for attending" email that everyone ignores.

CRISP Breakdown

Role You are a demand generation specialist who treats post-webinar email as the highest-leverage moment in the webinar funnel — this is when interest is highest and the conversion window is short.
Instruction Write two emails: Email 1 (to attendees, same day) — thanks + recording + primary CTA. Email 2 (to no-shows, next day) — re-engage with a hook from the webinar content + recording.
Spec Two separate emails. Each 150–200 words. Attendee email: personal, specific, one CTA. No-show email: starts with the most compelling insight from the webinar (not "sorry you missed it").
Performance Attendee email open rate potential: subject line references the webinar specifically. No-show email open rate potential: subject line gives them a reason to open even though they missed it. Both have one clear CTA.

Full Prompt — copy, fill placeholders, paste into Claude.ai

Context: I ran a webinar titled "[Webinar Title]" on [date]. Topic: [brief description of what was covered]. 
- Attendees: [N] people attended live
- No-shows: [N] registered but didn't attend
- Top 2–3 insights from the webinar: [list the most valuable things covered — what attendees said was most useful]
- Resources to share: [recording link / slides / template / guide]
- Primary next step I want people to take: [free trial / book a demo / read blog post / download resource]
- Company: [name and brief description]

- Does NOT open with "sorry you missed it"
- Opens with the most compelling insight from the webinar (give them value immediately)

- One clear CTA (same as attendee email)

Spec:
- Two separate emails, clearly labelled
- 150–200 words each
- Subject lines that don't say "Recording available" or "Missed the webinar?"
- One CTA per email
- Attendee email: personal and specific about what was discussed
- No-show email: leads with value, not guilt

Performance bar: Attendee subject line makes them want to open even though they were there. No-show subject line makes them want to open even though they missed it. Both have a single clear next step.
