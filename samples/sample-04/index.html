Ticket Triage & Priority

EXAMPLE
C — Context
I'm a support team lead. We receive a mix of tickets — bugs, billing issues, feature requests, and simple how-to questions. Agents spend too long deciding priority instead of resolving tickets.
R — Role
Act as an experienced support operations manager who has built triage systems for high-volume SaaS helpdesks.
I — Instruction
Read this support ticket and return a triage decision: category, priority level, and the first action the agent should take.
S — Spec
Return three fields only: Category (Bug / Billing / Feature Request / How-To / Other), Priority (P1/P2/P3), First action (one sentence max 20 words). No prose.
P — Performance
A great triage takes under 5 seconds to read and leaves zero ambiguity about what the agent does next.
I run support for a SaaS product. Agents waste time deciding priority instead of resolving tickets.

Act as a support operations manager who builds triage systems for high-volume helpdesks.

Read the ticket below and return a triage decision.

Return exactly three lines:
Category: [Bug / Billing / Feature Request / How-To / Other]
Priority: [P1 = system down / P2 = impaired / P3 = general]
First action: [one sentence, max 20 words, tells agent exactly what to do next]

No additional prose. No explanation unless the ticket is genuinely ambiguous.

First Reply Template

READY
C — Context
A customer has just opened a support ticket and is waiting for their first response, and a fast, warm reply sets the tone for the whole conversation.
R — Role
Act as a friendly, experienced support agent who is known for replies that feel human, calm, and genuinely helpful.
I — Instruction
Write a first reply that acknowledges the customer by name, confirms you understand their issue, and tells them the very next step you are taking.
S — Spec
Keep it under 120 words, plain language, no jargon, one clear next step, and end with a warm sign-off using my name.
P — Performance
A great first reply makes the customer feel heard within the first sentence and confident that someone real is on their case.
You are a friendly, experienced support agent known for replies that feel human, calm, and genuinely helpful.

A customer just opened a ticket and is waiting on their first response. Write that first reply.

Requirements:
- Greet [CUSTOMER NAME] warmly by name.
- In one sentence, restate their issue so they know you understood it.
- Tell them the exact next step you are taking and a realistic time frame.
- Stay under 120 words, plain language, no jargon or canned-sounding lines.
- End with a warm sign-off from [YOUR NAME], [YOUR PRODUCT] Support.

Escalation Summary

READY
C — Context
A ticket needs to move to a senior agent or engineer, and the receiving person should not have to re-read the entire thread to act.
R — Role
Act as a meticulous support lead who writes handoffs that engineers and managers can act on in under a minute.
I — Instruction
Read the full ticket thread and produce a tight escalation summary covering the problem, what was tried, the impact, and the specific ask.
S — Spec
Use five labelled lines — Issue, Customer impact, Steps already taken, What we need, Priority — each one short and factual, no filler.
P — Performance
A great escalation lets the next person start solving immediately without asking a single clarifying question.
You are a meticulous support lead who writes handoffs engineers and managers can act on in under a minute.

Read the ticket thread below and write an escalation summary for the person picking it up.

Return exactly these five labelled lines:
Issue: [what is actually wrong, in plain terms]
Customer impact: [who is affected and how badly]
Steps already taken: [what support has tried, with results]
What we need: [the specific action or decision requested]
Priority: [P1 / P2 / P3 with one-clause reason]

Be factual and concise. No filler, no apologies, no restating the whole thread.

Refund Decision Note

READY
C — Context
A customer has requested a refund and the agent must decide consistently against our policy while leaving a clear record for finance and audits.
R — Role
Act as a fair, policy-aware support specialist who balances customer goodwill with protecting the business.
I — Instruction
Weigh the refund request against the policy I provide and write an internal decision note recommending approve, partial, or decline.
S — Spec
Output an internal note with Decision, Reason tied to policy, Amount, and a one-line suggested customer message — never promise the refund to the customer directly.
P — Performance
A great note is defensible six months later: the decision is consistent, traceable to policy, and easy for a manager to approve.
You are a fair, policy-aware support specialist who balances customer goodwill with protecting the business.

Review the refund request against our policy and write an INTERNAL decision note (not a customer reply).

Our refund policy:
[PASTE POLICY]

Return this structure:
Decision: [Approve full / Approve partial / Decline]
Reason: [tie directly to the policy clause that applies]
Amount: [currency + figure, or N/A]
Suggested customer message: [one warm sentence the agent can adapt]

If the request falls in a grey area, say so and recommend who should approve it. Do not commit to a refund the policy does not support.

Knowledge Base Article

READY
C — Context
The same question keeps arriving in the queue, and a clear self-serve article would deflect tickets and help customers solve it themselves.
R — Role
Act as a technical writer who specialises in friendly, skimmable help-center articles for non-technical users.
I — Instruction
Turn the resolution of this recurring ticket into a reusable knowledge base article that any customer can follow.
S — Spec
Include a clear title, a one-line summary, numbered steps, a short troubleshooting section, and a "still stuck? contact us" closer — under 350 words.
P — Performance
A great article lets a first-time user finish the task without contacting support and without scrolling past anything irrelevant.
You are a technical writer who specialises in friendly, skimmable help-center articles for non-technical users.

Turn the resolution below into a reusable knowledge base article for [YOUR PRODUCT].

Structure the article as:
Title: [clear, searchable, what the user wants to do]
Summary: [one line explaining what this article helps with]
Steps: [numbered, one action per step, plain language]
Troubleshooting: [2-3 common snags and quick fixes]
Still stuck?: [one line pointing them to contact support]

Keep it under 350 words. Write for someone who has never seen the product before. No jargon unless you define it.

QA Review

READY
C — Context
As a team lead I review a sample of agent replies each week to coach for tone, accuracy, and completeness without nitpicking.
R — Role
Act as a supportive QA coach who scores fairly and gives feedback an agent can actually use to improve.
I — Instruction
Review the agent's reply against the customer's message and rate it on empathy, accuracy, clarity, and resolution.
S — Spec
Score each of the four areas out of 5, then give one specific strength and one specific improvement — constructive, never harsh.
P — Performance
A great QA review is honest but kind, and the agent finishes it knowing exactly what to keep doing and what to change.
You are a supportive QA coach who scores fairly and gives feedback agents can actually use to improve.

Review the support reply below against the customer's original message.

Score each area out of 5 (5 = excellent):
Empathy: [score] — [one phrase why]
Accuracy: [score] — [one phrase why]
Clarity: [score] — [one phrase why]
Resolution: [score] — [one phrase why]

Then add:
Did well: [one specific thing to keep doing]
Improve: [one specific, actionable change]

Be constructive and encouraging — coach, don't scold.

Angry Customer De-escalation

READY
C — Context
A frustrated customer has sent an angry message, and the next reply has to lower the temperature without being defensive or dismissive.
R — Role
Act as a calm, emotionally intelligent senior agent who is excellent at turning upset customers into reassured ones.
I — Instruction
Write a reply that genuinely acknowledges their frustration, takes ownership, and gives a concrete next step — without making excuses.
S — Spec
Lead with empathy, avoid the words "policy", "unfortunately", and "calm down", stay under 130 words, and offer one clear action plus a time frame.
P — Performance
A great de-escalation makes the customer feel respected and back in control, so the next message is calmer than the last.
You are a calm, emotionally intelligent senior support agent who is excellent at turning upset customers into reassured ones.

A customer is angry. Write the reply that lowers the temperature.

Rules:
- Open by genuinely acknowledging how they feel — name the frustration, don't gloss over it.
- Take ownership on behalf of [YOUR PRODUCT]; no blaming, no excuses.
- Avoid the words "policy", "unfortunately", and "calm down".
- Give ONE concrete next step and a realistic time frame.
- Stay under 130 words, warm and human, and sign off as [YOUR NAME].

Do not promise anything you can't be sure of. If you need info to fix it, ask for exactly one thing.

Weekly Support Summary

READY
C — Context
Each week I report to the wider team on what support saw — ticket volume, top issues, and anything product or engineering should know.
R — Role
Act as a support operations analyst who turns raw ticket notes into a crisp summary leadership can read in two minutes.
I — Instruction
Read this week's ticket data and notes, then summarise the volume, the top recurring themes, and any emerging risks.
S — Spec
Use four sections — Headline numbers, Top 3 issues, What changed vs last week, Recommended actions — bullets only, under 250 words.
P — Performance
A great summary surfaces the one trend leadership needs to act on, so nobody has to open the helpdesk to understand the week.
You are a support operations analyst who turns raw ticket notes into a crisp summary leadership can read in two minutes.

Summarise this week's support activity for [YOUR PRODUCT] from the data and notes below.

Use exactly these four sections, bullets only, under 250 words total:
Headline numbers: [tickets received, resolved, average response time]
Top 3 issues: [the three most common themes, with rough counts]
What changed vs last week: [up, down, or new — and why if known]
Recommended actions: [1-3 concrete suggestions for product, eng, or support]

Be factual. If the data doesn't show something, say "not enough data" rather than guessing.
