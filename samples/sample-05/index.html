1:1 Agenda Generator

EXAMPLE
C — Context
I'm a people manager with a team of 6. I run weekly 1:1s but often start with a blank agenda and the conversation drifts. I want structured agendas tailored to each person's current situation.
R — Role
Act as an experienced people manager and executive coach who designs 1:1 structures that balance task updates with career development and wellbeing.
I — Instruction
Generate a 30-minute 1:1 agenda for a team member based on the context I give you about their current situation and recent events.
S — Spec
Return 4–5 agenda items with a time allocation for each. Include at least one career/growth topic and one wellbeing check-in. Format as a numbered list. Total must add to 30 minutes.
P — Performance
A great agenda gives the manager a clear through-line for the conversation — not a generic template but something that reflects this person's actual situation right now.
I'm a people manager running weekly 1:1s. I need a structured 30-minute agenda that fits the team member's current situation — not a generic template.

Act as an experienced people manager and executive coach who designs 1:1 structures balancing task updates, career development, and wellbeing.

Generate a 30-minute 1:1 agenda based on the team member context below.

Format:
- Numbered list of 4–5 items
- Each item: topic + time allocation (e.g. "2. Project blockers — 8 min")
- Include at least one career/growth topic and one wellbeing check
- Total must add to exactly 30 minutes

Team member context: [DESCRIBE their current project, any known stressors, recent wins, or open topics]

Performance Review Draft

READY
C — Context
It's review season and I manage several people, but my notes are scattered across 1:1 logs, project retros, and Slack — I need help turning raw observations into a fair, evidence-based written review.
R — Role
Act as a seasoned people manager and HR partner who writes balanced performance reviews grounded in specific evidence rather than vague praise or criticism.
I — Instruction
Turn the notes I paste below into a first-draft performance review covering strengths, areas for growth, and impact over the review period.
S — Spec
Use four headed sections (Summary, Key Strengths, Growth Areas, Goals for Next Period). Tie every point to a concrete example from my notes, keep the tone professional and warm, and flag anything you had to assume.
P — Performance
A great draft reads like I wrote it, is specific enough that the report recognises themselves in it, and never invents achievements I didn't mention.
I'm a people manager writing a performance review and my notes are scattered. Help me turn raw observations into a fair, evidence-based draft.

Act as a seasoned people manager and HR partner who writes balanced reviews grounded in specific evidence — not vague praise or criticism.

Turn the notes below into a first-draft performance review for the review period.

Format:
- Four sections: Summary, Key Strengths, Growth Areas, Goals for Next Period
- Tie every point to a concrete example from my notes
- Professional, warm tone
- At the end, list any assumptions you had to make and any gaps where you need more input from me
- Do NOT invent achievements or numbers I didn't provide

Report name: [REPORT NAME]
Role / level: [ROLE]
Review period: [e.g. Jan–Jun 2026]
My raw notes (1:1s, project outcomes, feedback received): [PASTE NOTES]

Constructive Feedback Framer

READY
C — Context
I need to give a team member feedback about something that isn't working, and I tend to either soften it until the message is lost or come across harsher than I intend.
R — Role
Act as a calm, experienced manager and communication coach who frames difficult feedback so it's clear, specific, and easy to act on without damaging the relationship.
I — Instruction
Help me rewrite the situation I describe into constructive feedback using a behaviour → impact → request structure that I can deliver in a 1:1.
S — Spec
Give me (1) a 3–4 sentence script using the SBI model and (2) two open questions to invite their perspective. Focus on observable behaviour, avoid labels like "lazy" or "difficult", and keep it respectful.
P — Performance
Great feedback names the specific behaviour and its impact, makes a clear ask, and leaves the person feeling respected and clear on the next step — not attacked.
I'm a people manager who needs to give a team member some difficult feedback, and I want it to be clear and humane — not vague, not harsh.

Act as a calm, experienced manager and communication coach who frames feedback so it's specific, fair, and easy to act on without damaging trust.

Rewrite the situation below into constructive feedback I can deliver in a 1:1.

Format:
- A 3–4 sentence script using Situation–Behaviour–Impact (SBI), describing observable behaviour only
- Make one clear, reasonable request
- Then two open questions that invite their side of the story
- Avoid character labels (e.g. "lazy", "difficult"); stay respectful and assume good intent

Who: [REPORT NAME], [ROLE]
What happened (facts only): [DESCRIBE THE BEHAVIOUR AND ITS IMPACT]
Outcome I'm hoping for: [WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE]

Goal-Setting Session

READY
C — Context
I'm sitting down with a team member to set goals for the next quarter, and I want goals that are ambitious but realistic and tied to both business needs and their own growth.
R — Role
Act as a manager and performance coach who helps shape vague intentions into clear, measurable goals the team member feels ownership over.
I — Instruction
Take the rough priorities I describe and draft 3–4 quarterly goals, each written in SMART form with a suggested measure of success.
S — Spec
For each goal give: a one-line title, a SMART statement, how we'll measure it, and one possible first step. Mix delivery goals with at least one development goal. Keep it to one page.
P — Performance
Great goals are specific enough to track, motivating rather than imposed, and balanced between what the business needs and what helps the person grow.
I'm a people manager preparing a quarterly goal-setting session and I want goals that are ambitious, measurable, and motivating — not boilerplate.

Act as a manager and performance coach who turns rough priorities into clear goals the team member feels ownership over.

Draft 3–4 quarterly goals from the priorities below.

Format, for each goal:
- One-line title
- SMART statement (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound)
- How we'll measure success
- One suggested first step
Include at least one growth/development goal alongside delivery goals. Keep the whole thing to about one page.

Team member: [REPORT NAME], [ROLE]
Their rough priorities / things they want to work on: [PASTE NOTES]
Business priorities this quarter: [DESCRIBE]

Coaching Question Set

READY
C — Context
A team member is stuck on a problem and my instinct is to jump in with answers, but I want to coach them to think it through themselves.
R — Role
Act as a professional coach trained in the GROW model who helps managers ask, not tell, so their people build problem-solving confidence.
I — Instruction
Generate a set of open coaching questions I can use in a 1:1 to help the person work through the challenge I describe.
S — Spec
Group 8–10 questions under the GROW headings (Goal, Reality, Options, Will). Make them open (no yes/no), non-leading, and free of hidden advice. Add one line on how to listen between questions.
P — Performance
A great set helps the person reach their own insight and next step — the manager talks less than they do and never sneaks advice in as a question.
I'm a people manager and a team member is stuck. I want to coach them to solve it rather than hand them the answer.

Act as a professional coach trained in the GROW model who helps managers ask, not tell.

Generate a coaching question set I can use in a 1:1 for the challenge below.

Format:
- 8–10 questions grouped under GROW: Goal, Reality, Options, Will
- All questions open-ended (no yes/no), non-leading, with no hidden advice
- End with one tip on how to listen and follow up between questions

The challenge they're facing: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION]
What I'd love them to walk away with: [e.g. a clear next step they chose themselves]

Promotion Case Writer

READY
C — Context
I believe a team member is ready for promotion and I need to write a persuasive, evidence-backed case for a calibration or promotion committee.
R — Role
Act as a manager and HR business partner who writes promotion cases that map a person's real impact against the expectations of the next level.
I — Instruction
Turn the evidence I provide into a structured promotion case arguing why this person is already operating at the next level.
S — Spec
Use sections: Summary recommendation, Evidence of next-level impact (3–4 examples), Scope & complexity, Peer/stakeholder signals, and Any gaps to address. Keep claims tied to facts I give you and quantify impact where possible.
P — Performance
A strong case shows the person is consistently working at the next level — not just performing well at their current one — using specific, verifiable examples a committee can trust.
I'm a people manager building a promotion case for a committee and I want it persuasive and evidence-backed — not a list of adjectives.

Act as a manager and HR business partner who maps a person's real impact against the expectations of the next level.

Turn the evidence below into a structured promotion case.

Format:
- Summary recommendation (2–3 sentences)
- Evidence of next-level impact: 3–4 specific examples
- Scope & complexity they handle today
- Peer / stakeholder signals
- Any gaps to address before or after promotion
Quantify impact wherever I've given numbers; do not invent metrics. Argue why they ALREADY operate at the next level, not just that they do their current job well.

Person: [REPORT NAME]
Current level: [CURRENT ROLE] → Proposed level: [TARGET ROLE]
Next-level expectations (if I have them): [PASTE LADDER / LEAVE BLANK]
Evidence — achievements, scope, feedback, metrics: [PASTE NOTES]

PIP Summary

READY
C — Context
Performance concerns with a team member have not improved after informal conversations, and I need to draft a fair, clear performance improvement plan summary before involving HR.
R — Role
Act as an experienced manager and HR partner who writes performance documentation that is specific, factual, humane, and focused on giving the person a genuine path to succeed.
I — Instruction
Turn the concerns and examples I provide into a structured PIP summary stating the gaps, the expected standard, and concrete support and milestones.
S — Spec
Use sections: Areas of concern (with dated examples), Expected standard, Support offered, Review milestones & timeline, and Consequences if not met. Keep the tone neutral and non-punitive, describe behaviour not character, and remind me to have HR review it.
P — Performance
A fair PIP is specific and factual, sets a clearly measurable bar, offers real support, and reads as a sincere chance to improve — never as a paper trail for dismissal.
I'm a people manager drafting a performance improvement plan after informal coaching hasn't closed the gap. I want it fair, specific, and humane — a real chance to succeed, not a paper trail.

Act as an experienced manager and HR partner who writes performance documentation that is factual, respectful, and focused on a genuine path to improvement.

Turn the concerns and examples below into a structured PIP summary.

Format:
- Areas of concern — each with at least one dated, observable example
- Expected standard — what "good" looks like, measurably
- Support offered — training, coaching, resources, check-in cadence
- Review milestones & timeline (e.g. 30 / 60 / 90 days)
- Consequences if the standard isn't met, stated plainly and without threat

Rules: describe behaviour and impact, not character; use only facts I provide (don't invent incidents); keep the tone neutral and non-punitive. End by reminding me to have HR/legal review this before sharing.

Team member: [REPORT NAME], [ROLE]
Concerns and specific examples (with dates): [PASTE NOTES]
Support I can realistically offer: [DESCRIBE]
Proposed review period: [e.g. 60 days]

Team Health Check

READY
C — Context
I want a regular read on how my team is really doing — morale, workload, collaboration, and clarity — beyond what surfaces in standups.
R — Role
Act as a manager and team-effectiveness coach who designs lightweight pulse checks that surface real signals without feeling like surveillance.
I — Instruction
Design a short team health-check survey plus a plan for how I read and act on the results.
S — Spec
Produce 6–8 questions across morale, workload, clarity, collaboration, and growth — mostly 1–5 scale with one or two open questions. Add a short note on keeping it anonymous and what I should do with low scores. Keep it under 5 minutes to complete.
P — Performance
A great health check is quick, psychologically safe, and gives me honest, actionable signal — and the follow-up plan makes sure the team sees something change as a result.
I'm a people manager who wants a regular, honest read on how my team is doing — morale, workload, clarity, collaboration, growth — beyond standups.

Act as a manager and team-effectiveness coach who designs lightweight pulse checks that surface real signals without feeling like surveillance.

Design a team health-check survey plus a plan to act on it.

Format:
- 6–8 questions covering morale, workload, clarity, collaboration, and growth
- Mostly 1–5 scale, with one or two open-text questions
- A short note on keeping responses anonymous and safe
- A "what to do next" section: how to read the results and how to respond to low scores so the team sees real follow-up
Keep completion time under 5 minutes.

Team size: [NUMBER]
Anything specific I'm worried about right now: [DESCRIBE, OR "nothing specific"]
How often I plan to run it: [e.g. monthly]
