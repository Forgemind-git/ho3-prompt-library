# Prompt 02 — LinkedIn Thought Leadership Post

**Library:** Content Marketer Prompt Library  
**Role:** Content Marketing Manager

---

## Purpose

Write a LinkedIn post that builds genuine professional authority on a topic relevant to your audience — without self-promotion, humble-bragging, or the kind of vague inspirational content that LinkedIn is already drowning in.

---

## CRISP Breakdown

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Context** | Author: [name, title, company]. Topic/angle: [specific insight or contrarian point of view]. Audience: [who you want to reach on LinkedIn]. Goal: [brand awareness / lead generation / talent attraction / community building]. |
| **Role** | You are a ghostwriter who specialises in LinkedIn content for B2B founders and executives — you write posts that sound like the person, not like a LinkedIn engagement pod. |
| **Instruction** | Write a LinkedIn post that opens with a hook that earns the scroll, develops one clear idea with a specific insight, and closes with a genuine question or takeaway — not a CTA to DM or visit a link. |
| **Spec** | LinkedIn format. 200–280 words. No bullet-point lists in the first 3 lines (LinkedIn hides them). Hook in the first line — no "I've been thinking about..." openings. No emoji overuse (max 2). No "drop your thoughts in the comments" close. |
| **Performance** | Someone in the target audience reads this and thinks "that's exactly what I've been thinking but couldn't articulate." The post feels like it was written by a real person, not a content machine. |

---

## Full Prompt

```
Context: I'm writing a LinkedIn thought leadership post for [Author Name], [Title] at [Company]. 
- Topic/angle: [the specific insight, observation, or contrarian view you want to share — be specific, not "leadership tips"]
- Supporting evidence or example: [a data point, personal experience, or specific story that makes the argument real]
- Target audience on LinkedIn: [who you want to reach — e.g., "VPs of Customer Success at mid-market SaaS companies"]
- Brand voice: [describe the author's style — e.g., "direct, data-driven, occasionally self-deprecating"]
- Goal of this post: [build authority / generate inbound / attract talent / start a conversation]

Role: Act as a ghostwriter who specialises in LinkedIn content for B2B executives. You write posts that sound like the person said it in a meeting — not like they asked an AI to write their LinkedIn.

Instruction: Write a LinkedIn post that:
1. Opens with a single hook sentence that creates enough tension to earn a "see more" click (not a question, not "I've been thinking about...")
2. Develops ONE idea with a specific angle — not a list of tips
3. Uses a concrete example or data point to make the argument real
4. Closes with either a genuine question OR one clear actionable takeaway — not a promotional CTA

Spec:
- 200–280 words
- No bullet lists in the first 3 lines
- Hook in line 1 — no warm-up
- Max 2 emoji total
- No "drop your thoughts below" or "DM me if you want to chat"
- Written in first person, author's voice

Performance bar: Target audience member reads this and thinks "this is exactly what I've been thinking." Post feels like a human wrote it, not a content tool. No generic inspirational language.
```

---

## Before Example (Weak Prompt)

**Prompt used:**
> Write a LinkedIn post about customer success.

**Output produced:**
> Customer success is so important in today's competitive landscape! As a CS leader, I've learned that putting the customer first is always the right move. Here are 5 tips for better customer success: 1. Listen to customers. 2. Be proactive. 3. Build relationships. 4. Use data. 5. Celebrate wins! What are your top CS tips? Drop them below!

**Why it's weak:** Generic enough to be written by anyone. "Listen to customers" is not an insight. Five tips with zero evidence. "Drop them below" is a hollow engagement bait close. Nobody reads this and thinks "I should follow this person."

---

## After Example (With CRISP)

**Placeholders filled:**
- Author: Sarah Chen, VP Customer Success at Helply
- Angle: Companies that use CSAT surveys monthly are measuring the wrong thing — CSAT is a lagging indicator that tells you about service quality, not retention health
- Evidence: Helply's internal data — customers who churn gave 4+ star CSAT scores in their last 90 days
- Audience: CS leaders at B2B SaaS companies
- Voice: Direct, data-driven, comfortable challenging conventional wisdom

**Output produced:**

> Your customers are giving you 5-star CSAT scores and then churning 90 days later.
>
> We see this pattern constantly. Support interaction is excellent. The team is responsive, helpful, professional. CSAT scores are the highest they've ever been.
>
> And then the renewal conversation happens.
>
> Here's what I think is happening: CSAT measures how customers feel about service interactions. It doesn't measure whether they're getting value from the product. These are completely different things, and we keep conflating them.
>
> A customer who had a frustrating experience that got resolved well will give you a 5. A customer who is quietly not achieving their core use case — but hasn't had any support issues — will also give you a 5. One of those customers is at serious churn risk and your CSAT score will not tell you which one.
>
> The signal we've found more predictive is product engagement patterns against the customer's stated goal at onboarding. Not "are they logging in?" but "are they logging in to do the thing they bought the product to do?"
>
> CSAT is useful for measuring support quality. It's a poor proxy for retention health.
>
> What leading indicator has your team found most predictive of renewals?

**Why it works:** Opens with a specific tension, makes a clear contrarian argument, uses real evidence, closes with a genuine question that will actually get CS leaders to respond with their own data.
