# Prompt 01 — Blog Post Outline

**Library:** Content Marketer Prompt Library  
**Role:** Content Marketing Manager

---

## Purpose

Create a SEO-structured blog post outline with H2/H3 hierarchy, estimated section word counts, and notes for each section — so a writer can produce a complete draft without needing to think about structure, and every section earns its place.

---

## CRISP Breakdown

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Context** | Topic: [blog post topic]. Target keyword: [primary keyword]. Search intent: [informational / commercial / navigational]. Target audience: [who reads this and what problem are they solving]. Brand: [company name, voice — e.g., "authoritative but not stuffy"]. |
| **Role** | You are an SEO content strategist who builds outlines that rank AND convert — you don't optimise for Google at the expense of the reader, or for the reader at the expense of ranking. |
| **Instruction** | Create a detailed blog post outline with: SEO title options (3), intro hook approach, H2 sections with H3 sub-sections, section notes, and a conclusion with CTA approach. |
| **Spec** | Outline format. Total target word count: [800 / 1200 / 2000 words]. Each H2 has an estimated word count allocation. Section notes tell the writer what to include, not how to write it. |
| **Performance** | A writer with no additional briefing can produce a complete first draft from this outline. The keyword appears in the title, meta description hint, and at least 2 H2s naturally. |

---

## Full Prompt

```
Context: I'm a content marketing manager at [Company Name], a [brief company description — what you do and for whom]. I'm writing a blog post on: [topic]. 
- Primary target keyword: [keyword]
- Secondary keywords: [list 2–3]
- Search intent: [informational — "how to X" / commercial — "best X" / comparison — "X vs Y"]
- Target audience: [describe who reads this and what problem they're trying to solve]
- Ideal reader outcome: [what should the reader be able to do or understand after reading?]
- Brand voice: [describe tone — e.g., "practical, direct, no fluff; expertise without jargon"]
- Word count target: [800 / 1200 / 2000 words]
- Any competitors ranking for this topic I should be aware of: [list or "I'll handle this separately"]

Role: Act as an SEO content strategist who builds outlines that rank for the target keyword AND genuinely help the reader — not keyword-stuffed content that embarrasses the brand.

Instruction: Create a blog post outline with:
1. Three SEO title options — one question-based, one "how to", one direct/list format
2. Meta description hint — one sentence summary for the writer to refine
3. Intro — describe the hook approach (problem / statistic / story / counter-intuitive claim) and what the intro must accomplish
4. H2 Sections — 4–6 H2s with: title, estimated word count, H3 sub-sections where needed, and 2–3 sentence notes for the writer
5. Conclusion — approach for the closing section and CTA type

Spec:
- Outline format (not a draft — section notes only)
- Word count must add up to the target
- Primary keyword in at least 2 H2 titles naturally
- Section notes: what to cover, not how to write it
- Include a "what to avoid" note for the most common mistake in this type of post

Performance bar: Writer produces a complete, on-target draft from this outline without any additional briefing. Keyword appears naturally (not forced) in title, 2 H2s, and first paragraph.
```

---

## Before Example (Weak Prompt)

**Prompt used:**
> Outline a blog post about project management tools.

**Output produced:**
> 1. Introduction 2. What are project management tools? 3. Top tools 4. How to choose 5. Conclusion

**Why it's weak:** No keyword strategy, no section notes, no word count allocation, no CTA approach. This outline gives a writer nothing to work with. The resulting post will be generic.

---

## After Example (With CRISP)

**Placeholders filled:**
- Company: Helply (B2B SaaS customer support platform)
- Topic: How small teams can manage customer support without hiring a full team
- Keyword: "customer support for small teams"
- Search intent: Informational + commercial
- Audience: Founders and ops leads at startups (5–30 people), frustrated by support chaos
- Voice: Practical, direct, honest about trade-offs
- Word count: 1,200

**Output produced:**

> **Blog Post Outline — Customer Support for Small Teams**
>
> **Title Options**
> 1. How to Handle Customer Support With a Small Team (Without Burning Out)
> 2. Customer Support for Small Teams: The Practical Playbook
> 3. 5 Things Small Teams Do Differently to Keep Customers Happy Without a Support Department
>
> **Meta Description Hint:**  
> "Small team, big support expectations? Here's how to build a system that handles volume without hiring a dedicated team — including the tools that actually make a difference."
>
> ---
>
> **Intro (150 words) — Hook approach: Problem + Validation**  
> Open with the specific frustration: the founder answering support emails at midnight, customer complaints falling through the cracks, "we'll get back to you" becoming a lie. Then validate that this is a solvable problem — small teams can punch above their weight on support if they build the right system. End with a clear statement of what this post will cover.
>
> ---
>
> **H2 #1: Why Customer Support Breaks at 20+ Customers (200 words)**  
> *Notes:* Explain the inflection point where informal support stops working — no one owns it, tickets get lost, response times balloon. Use a concrete example scenario. Don't solve anything here — build the problem before the solution. Include "customer support for small teams" naturally.
>
> **H2 #2: The System That Works for Small Teams (250 words)**  
> *Notes:* Introduce the framework: single inbox, triage, templates, escalation path. This is the core value section. Be specific — name the actual components. 
> - **H3: One Inbox to Rule Them All** — why centralisation is the first step
> - **H3: Triage Without a Triage Person** — how to use tags/labels to prioritise
>
> **H2 #3: Tools Worth Using (and What to Skip) (200 words)**  
> *Notes:* 3–4 tools with a one-line description and a "best for" note. Be honest about limitations. Don't just list every tool — make a judgment call.
>
> **H2 #4: Response Templates That Don't Sound Robotic (200 words)**  
> *Notes:* Give 2–3 example templates or template types. Explain why templates save time without killing the human touch when done right.
>
> **H2 #5: When to Hire vs When to Automate (150 words)**  
> *Notes:* Honest assessment of the decision point. When does the system break down and hiring becomes the right move?
>
> ---
>
> **Conclusion (50 words) — CTA: Free trial or content download**  
> Summarise in 2 sentences what the reader now has. CTA: "Ready to centralise your support without adding headcount? [Company] helps small teams handle support at scale — try it free."
>
> **What to Avoid:** Don't write a "tools comparison" post in disguise. This should be a system/process post that happens to mention tools — not the other way around.
