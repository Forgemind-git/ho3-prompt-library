Prospect Research Brief

EXAMPLE
C — Context
I'm an SDR preparing for a first outreach to a new prospect. I have their LinkedIn URL and company website but no prior contact history.
R — Role
Act as a B2B sales researcher who specialises in turning public information into actionable prospect intelligence.
I — Instruction
Summarise this prospect's likely pain points, recent company signals, and the strongest hook I can use in my opening line.
S — Spec
Return three bullet points: (1) Top pain point, (2) Recent signal, (3) Recommended hook. Maximum 40 words per bullet. No filler.
P — Performance
A great output gives me something specific enough to name in the email — not generic industry problems but this company's situation.

Cold Email First Line

READY
C — Context
I'm an SDR sending a cold email to a prospect who has never heard of us, and the opening line decides whether the rest of the email gets read.
R — Role
Act as a cold-email copywriter who writes personalised opening lines that sound human and reference something specific about the prospect.
I — Instruction
Write three different first-line options that hook this prospect by referencing their role, company, or a recent trigger event — not our product.
S — Spec
Each line is one sentence, under 20 words, written in a casual peer-to-peer tone, with no greeting, no "I hope this finds you well", and no pitch.
P — Performance
A great line feels hand-written for this person — they could not imagine it being sent to anyone else on my list.
Act as a cold-email copywriter who writes personalised opening lines that sound human, not templated.

- Casual, peer-to-peer tone

- Must feel hand-written for this specific person

Follow-up Sequence

READY
C — Context
I sent a first cold email to a prospect and got no reply, and I need a short follow-up sequence that stays useful instead of just "bumping" the thread.
R — Role
Act as a B2B sales sequence writer who knows that every follow-up must add a new reason to reply, not repeat the last one.
I — Instruction
Write a three-email follow-up sequence where each message takes a different angle: a useful resource, a relevant proof point, and a polite break-up email.
S — Spec
Each email is under 80 words with its own subject line and a single clear call to action, spaced across day 3, day 7, and day 12.
P — Performance
A great sequence never says "just following up" — every email could stand alone and still earn a reply from a busy buyer.
Act as a B2B sales sequence writer. Every follow-up must add a NEW reason to reply, never just bump the thread.

Context: I sent a first cold email and got no response. Write a three-email follow-up sequence.

Use a different angle for each email:
1. Day 3 — share a genuinely useful resource (article, benchmark, template)
2. Day 7 — a short, relevant proof point or customer result
3. Day 12 — a polite break-up email that makes it easy to say "not now"

Rules:
- Each email under 80 words
- Each has its own subject line and ONE clear call to action
- Never write "just following up" or "circling back"

Prospect name: [PROSPECT NAME]
Company: [COMPANY]
What I sell: [YOUR PRODUCT]
The problem it solves for them: [PAIN POINT]
Best proof point I have: [CASE STUDY / RESULT]

Objection Reframe

READY
C — Context
A prospect just pushed back on my outreach with an objection, and I need a calm, confident reply that keeps the conversation open instead of arguing.
R — Role
Act as a sales coach who teaches reps to acknowledge the objection, reframe it, and ask one question that moves the deal forward.
I — Instruction
Turn the prospect's objection into a short reply that validates their concern, offers a new way to see it, and ends with a low-pressure question.
S — Spec
Reply in three short sentences, under 60 words total, with a warm and non-defensive tone, and absolutely no "but" at the start of the reframe.
P — Performance
A great reframe makes the prospect feel heard first, so they stay curious and reply rather than feeling sold to or corrected.
Act as a sales coach. Teach me to handle this objection by acknowledging it, reframing it, and asking ONE question that keeps the deal moving.

3. Ends with a low-pressure question

Rules:
- Three short sentences, under 60 words total
- Warm, confident, never defensive
- Make them feel heard before you reframe

Call Prep Sheet

READY
C — Context
I have a discovery call booked with a prospect in 30 minutes and need a one-page prep sheet so I walk in sharp instead of winging it.
R — Role
Act as a senior sales manager who preps reps for discovery calls and knows which questions actually surface budget, pain, and timeline.
I — Instruction
Build a focused call prep sheet with likely pain points, four strong discovery questions, two probable objections, and a clear next-step ask.
S — Spec
Use clear headers and short bullets that fit on one screen, written so I can scan them mid-call without losing the conversation.
P — Performance
A great prep sheet gives me questions specific to this prospect's role and industry — not a generic discovery checklist I could find anywhere.
Act as a senior sales manager prepping me for a discovery call. I have 30 minutes before it starts.

Build a one-page call prep sheet with these sections:
1. LIKELY PAIN POINTS — 3 bullets specific to this role and industry
2. DISCOVERY QUESTIONS — 4 strong questions that surface budget, pain, and timeline
3. LIKELY OBJECTIONS — 2 they may raise, plus a one-line reframe for each
4. NEXT-STEP ASK — the specific commitment I should aim to close the call on

Rules:
- Short bullets, clear headers, fits on one screen
- Scannable mid-call
- Make it specific to this prospect, not a generic checklist

Prospect: [NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]
Industry: [INDUSTRY]
What I sell: [YOUR PRODUCT]
What I already know about them: [NOTES / RESEARCH]

Win/Loss Debrief

READY
C — Context
A deal just closed as a win or a loss, and I want to debrief it honestly so I repeat what worked and stop repeating what didn't.
R — Role
Act as a sales analyst who runs blameless win/loss reviews and separates what I controlled from what I never could.
I — Instruction
Analyse my notes on this deal and identify the key turning point, what I did well, what I'd change, and one habit to carry into the next deal.
S — Spec
Return four labelled sections — Turning Point, What Worked, What I'd Change, One Habit — each two or three bullets, direct and non-judgemental.
P — Performance
A great debrief gives me one concrete, repeatable change for my next deal — not vague advice like "build more rapport".
Act as a sales analyst running a blameless win/loss review. Separate what I controlled from what I never could.

Analyse my notes on a deal that just closed and return these four sections:
1. TURNING POINT — the moment the deal was won or lost
2. WHAT WORKED — 2-3 things I did well
3. WHAT I'D CHANGE — 2-3 things to do differently next time
4. ONE HABIT — a single repeatable change to carry into my next deal

Rules:

- Each section is 2-3 bullets
- End with ONE concrete change, never vague advice like "build more rapport"

Pipeline Update Summary

READY
C — Context
My manager wants a weekly pipeline update, and I have messy notes on each deal that I need to turn into a clear, scannable summary.
R — Role
Act as a sales operations lead who turns raw deal notes into a crisp pipeline report a busy manager can read in under a minute.
I — Instruction
Summarise my deals by stage, flag which ones are at risk or need help, and surface the single most important thing my manager should know.
S — Spec
Group deals under stage headers with one line each (company, value, next step), then a short "Needs attention" list and a one-line headline.
P — Performance
A great summary makes it obvious where my manager should spend their help — the risks and asks jump out without me explaining them live.
Act as a sales operations lead. Turn my messy deal notes into a weekly pipeline summary my manager can read in under a minute.

Produce the report in this structure:
1. HEADLINE — one line: the single most important thing this week
2. BY STAGE — group deals under stage headers (e.g. Qualifying, Demo, Proposal, Negotiation). One line per deal: company, value, next step + date
3. NEEDS ATTENTION — deals at risk or where I need my manager's help, with the specific ask

Rules:
- Scannable, short lines, no fluff
- Make risks and asks jump out
- Keep my numbers exactly as I give them

My deals this week (raw notes): [PASTE NOTES — company, stage, value, last activity, next step, any blockers]
